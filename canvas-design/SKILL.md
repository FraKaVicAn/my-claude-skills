# Canvas Design Skill

Produce sophisticated visual artifacts where design carries the meaning. Text whispers; form, color, and composition communicate.

## When to Activate

- Creating a visual piece that should feel like designed art, not a decorated document
- Presenting a concept through visual language rather than prose
- Building a cover, poster, infographic, or abstract composition

## Two-Phase Process

### Phase 1 — Design Philosophy (.md)
Write a 4–6 paragraph aesthetic manifesto that:
- Names the visual movement (e.g., "Sparse Meridian", "Thermal Minimalism")
- Articulates how form, space, color, and composition carry meaning
- Describes the emotional or conceptual thread
- References a specific discipline or cultural touchpoint subtly — "like a jazz musician quoting another song"
- Emphasizes painstaking craft and master-level execution

### Phase 2 — Canvas Expression (.pdf or .png)
Translate the philosophy into a single-page artifact:
- **90% visual, 10% text** — text is a visual element, not explanation
- Repeating patterns and systematic visual language
- Sparse, clinical typography positioned with intention
- Professional spacing — no overlaps, no elements falling off-page
- Appears as if refined through countless iterations

## Design Principles

- "Information lives in design, not paragraphs"
- Typography as visual accent, never explanatory
- Negative space is active, not empty
- Colors chosen for symbolic or emotional resonance, not decoration
- The sophisticated viewer recognizes the reference; others experience pure composition

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Decorating text with design
- Using more than 2 typefaces
- Centering everything without reason
- Filling space out of discomfort with emptiness
